// 01 — The Big Picture

What Is Actually Moving This Market

The US-Iran war that began February 28, 2026 remains the single dominant force shaping every major asset class right now. Everything — indices, oil, gold, bonds, the dollar — is being priced relative to one question: when does the Strait of Hormuz reopen and how long does this last?

After weeks of sharp selling, markets staged their first winning week since the conflict began this past week. The S&P 500 gained 3.4%, the Nasdaq surged 4.4%, and the Dow added nearly 3%. The catalyst was optimism that Iran and the US were moving closer to a resolution — with reports that Iran and Oman were developing a protocol to monitor Hormuz shipping, and Trump hinting the war was "getting very close" to an end.

But that optimism was shaken late Wednesday when Trump gave a nationally televised address vowing to hit Iran "extremely hard" over the next two to three weeks with no clear exit timetable. Oil spiked back above $110 a barrel. Futures cratered overnight. By Thursday's close, stocks had recovered to finish flat to slightly positive — suggesting markets are starting to "numb" to the oil-headline cycle. That said, volatility is far from over.

// Key Risk This Week

Trump's rhetoric suggests ground operations could escalate over the Easter holiday weekend. JPMorgan's former chief strategist Kolanovic warned publicly against going long into the break. The first full week of April will open with markets repricing whatever happened over the weekend.

// 02 — US Indices: NQ / ES / YM

Equities: Cautious Recovery, Not a Reversal

The rally this week was significant but needs context. The Nasdaq remains down roughly 10% from its peak — technically in correction territory. The S&P 500 is hovering near correction territory as well. The Dow has been more resilient, supported by energy and industrial names that benefit from elevated oil prices.

The two-day rally at the end of March (Tuesday–Wednesday) was the S&P's best back-to-back gain since May. That kind of move in the context of an ongoing war signals that a segment of the market believes the worst of the equity selloff is behind us. But a sizeable camp disagrees — pointing to the fact that CPI data due Friday April 10 will be the first inflation print to actually show the war's impact. Consensus expects CPI to jump from 2.4% to 3.1% year-over-year. A hotter number would hit growth stocks hard.

The sectors to watch: energy stocks are outperforming sharply (XOM, CVX, OXY all up), while airlines, cruise operators, and consumer discretionary are lagging badly as fuel costs squeeze margins. Tech is mixed — Tesla down 5.5% on weak Q1 deliveries, while AI-adjacent names remain relatively supported.

// Bullish Factors

Jobs beat (178K vs 60K expected) signals economy not breaking. S&P posted first winning week. Iran-Oman Hormuz monitoring plan signals possible path to de-escalation. Market "numbing" to oil-headline cycle.

// Bearish Risks

CPI likely to spike Friday Apr 10. Trump ground operation risk over Easter. Nasdaq still in correction. Oil above $100 = persistent inflation pressure. Fed pricing in zero cuts for 2026. Duration of war = primary unknown.

// 03 — Crude Oil (CL)

Oil: The Engine of Everything Right Now

WTI crude settled at $111.54 per barrel Thursday — the highest since June 2022. Brent came in at $109.03. The move came entirely on Trump's Wednesday address signaling the war would continue for weeks and that the US would escalate strikes on Iranian infrastructure.

The Strait of Hormuz remains the core pressure point. Roughly 20% of the world's seaborne oil flows through it daily. With passage effectively blocked since the conflict began, the physical spot market has inverted — US crude is trading above Brent because buyers who need oil NOW are paying a premium for immediate delivery while June contracts price in some eventual resolution.

Gas prices at the pump have crossed $4 per gallon nationally for the first time in years. The IEA has warned supply will tighten further in April and is considering releasing strategic reserves. The war in oil is not just a trading story — it's a consumer inflation story that will show up in every economic data print for the next several months.

// Trader Note — Oil Correlation

Since the conflict began, oil and equities have moved inversely — oil up, stocks down and vice versa. That correlation softened this week as stocks rallied even while oil stayed elevated. Watch for a breakdown of that relationship as a potential signal of market regime change.

// 04 — Gold (GC)

Gold: The War Paradox — Down 13% Since Conflict Started

Gold's behavior since February 28 has confused most retail traders. A war broke out — and gold fell. Spot gold is down roughly 13% since the conflict began, posting its worst monthly performance since October 2008 in March. This is the opposite of conventional "safe haven" thinking.

The mechanism is straightforward once you understand it: the Iran war is driving oil prices higher → which feeds inflation expectations → which raises the probability of the Fed staying on hold or even hiking → which makes the dollar stronger and bond yields higher → which increases the opportunity cost of holding non-yielding gold → gold falls.

Additionally, gold had already run massively into early 2026. When a crowded trade meets a shock, the first instinct is to sell your winners to cover losses elsewhere. That's the liquidity flush. Goldman Sachs is maintaining its $5,400 year-end target, citing structural central bank buying as a floor. J.P. Morgan sees $6,300 by end of 2026. The case for gold long-term remains intact — but in this specific environment, war = dollar strength = gold headwind.

// Watch Level

De-escalation in Iran = dollar weakens = gold recovers fast. If Hormuz opens and rate-cut expectations return, analysts say gold could reclaim $5,000+ quickly. The war-end trade in gold may be one of the cleaner macro plays of 2026.

// 05 — The Federal Reserve

Fed: Hawkish Hold — Zero Cuts Now the Base Case

At the start of 2026, traders were pricing in two rate cuts. As of early April, the market is pricing zero cuts for the entire year — and some corners of the market are beginning to discuss whether a hike is back on the table. That's a dramatic repricing.

The Fed's current rate sits at 3.50%–3.75%. Jerome Powell's term expires in May 2026, and Trump has nominated Kevin Warsh as his replacement. Warsh was historically hawkish but has been signaling dovish leanings aligned with the administration's preference for lower rates. The problem: the committee itself is becoming more hawkish as oil-driven inflation erodes the case for cuts.

The March jobs report (178K vs 60K expected) further complicated the picture. A strong labor market gives the Fed zero urgency to cut. Schwab's analysis was blunt: "This report shouldn't change the Fed's near-term plans. We still expect an extended pause." The April 28–29 FOMC meeting is the next scheduled decision. Markets are pricing virtually zero probability of a move at that meeting.

This week's key Fed event: FOMC Minutes release on Wednesday April 8. Traders will be parsing for any shift in tone on inflation or any internal debate about hikes returning to the conversation.

// Fed Stance Summary

Hawkish hold. Fed is not cutting. Oil-driven inflation has killed the easing narrative for now. New chair Warsh will likely enter dovish but faces a hawkish committee. The pivot back to cuts requires either a sharp drop in oil prices (Hormuz opening) or a significant deterioration in the labor market. Neither is imminent.

// 06 — Trump & Policy Impact

Trump: War + Tariffs = Double Inflation Pressure

Beyond the Iran war, Trump's trade policy is creating a secondary layer of market uncertainty. This week he signed an executive order paving the way for tariffs of up to 100% on certain patented drugs if companies fail to reach agreements. He also reaffirmed plans for 50% duties on imported steel, aluminum, and copper.

The pharmaceutical tariff news hit healthcare stocks and adds to an already complex inflation picture. If tariffs push drug prices higher at the same time that energy costs are surging from the war, the consumer inflation story gets significantly worse — and the Fed's ability to cut without losing credibility gets even more constrained.

The broader Trump policy environment is driving what analysts are calling a "dual shock" narrative — geopolitical shock from the Iran war plus a domestic trade policy shock from escalating tariffs. For markets, this means higher structural inflation, a stronger dollar, and persistent uncertainty on growth. The bear case is a stagflation scenario: inflation stays high while economic growth slows under the weight of energy costs and trade friction.

// 08 — The Bottom Line

The Story of This Market in Plain English

The market is trading one macro story: Iran war duration and its inflation consequences. Every data point, every Trump speech, every Iran headline filters through that lens. The question the market asks every day is: does this bring us closer to Hormuz reopening, or does this push it further away?

Equities had their best week since the war began — but that was driven by hope, not resolution. The structural headwinds remain: oil above $100, zero Fed cuts priced in, CPI about to spike, and Trump's rhetoric suggesting continued escalation over Easter weekend. The bulls need a ceasefire or a credible de-escalation path. The bears need one more bad oil headline to push the Nasdaq back into its worst levels.

For traders: news-driven volatility is elevated. Sessions can gap and reverse on a single Trump post. Levels matter less than catalyst timing right now. Know what's on the calendar, know the oil price, and manage your size accordingly. This is not a market that rewards conviction bets without tight risk management.
