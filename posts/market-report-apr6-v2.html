What You Missed While the Market Was Closed

You stepped away after Thursday's close. The macro story is simple and brutal: the U.S.-Israel war with Iran — now in its sixth week — remains the single variable that controls every tick in NQ. Trump's April 1 primetime address promised more strikes. Iran vowed to fight back. Oil exploded through $111. Stocks took an intraday gut-punch Thursday before clawing back to flat. Then Friday dropped a jobs report that tripled expectations. The market wants to rally. Oil won't let it. That tension is exactly what you are walking into Monday morning.

🚨 Critical Background — The War That Is Running This Market

On February 28, 2026, the United States and Israel launched coordinated surprise airstrikes on Iran, assassinating Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei and multiple senior officials. Iran responded immediately with missile and drone strikes on U.S. bases, Gulf states, and Israeli territory — and closed the Strait of Hormuz, through which roughly 20% of the world's seaborne oil and significant LNG volumes normally flow. (Wikipedia — 2026 Iran War; Wikipedia — 2026 Strait of Hormuz Crisis)

The International Energy Agency characterized the resulting supply disruption as "the greatest global energy security challenge in history." The IEA, IMF, and World Bank formed a joint coordination group in response. (Reuters via StoneX Morning Commentary, Apr 2) Brent crude was trading near $73/bbl before the strikes. It has since surged roughly 80% to $112+. WTI is up 94% YTD. (Yahoo Finance CL=F; NBC News, Apr 2)

As of Sunday April 5, Trump has set an April 6 deadline for Iran to reopen the Strait of Hormuz or face strikes on Iran's power plants — a deadline he has already extended multiple times since March 23. (NPR, Mar 26; Gramercy EM Weekly, Apr 4; Newsquawk Week Ahead, Apr 5)

The Week — Day by Day

SUN MAR 30 –
MON MAR 31
Best Single Day Since May — Dow +1,100 Points

The week that preceded this report opened with the biggest single-day rally since last spring. On Sunday March 30, Trump told reporters at the White House that the U.S. would "probably" stop attacks on Iran within "two to three weeks, whether we have a deal or not." That was enough. Asian markets surged overnight — Tokyo's Nikkei 225 jumped 5.2%. By Monday's close, the Dow had surged more than 1,100 points, the S&P 500 gained 2.91% to close at 6,528.52, and the Nasdaq Composite advanced 3.83% to 21,590.63. All three major indexes recorded their best day since May 2025. Oil dropped roughly 4%, with Brent and WTI both briefly falling below $100 per barrel.

"The market tipped its hand," CNBC's Jim Cramer said. "When we got the rally Tuesday, you now know what happens when this war ends."

The White House announced Trump would address the nation Wednesday evening at 9 PM ET on the Iran war. Markets anticipated a victory declaration or ceasefire announcement. What they got was something very different.

Context: The three-month average for job growth in 2026 is running just above 68,000 per month — an improvement over 2025's 12,000-per-month pace but still below the historical average of 120,000. (CNN, Apr 3)

03 — The Iran Variable

Why NQ Cannot Trade Without Oil

The Strait of Hormuz has been effectively closed since March 2, 2026. A senior IRGC official confirmed the closure on March 2, threatening to set fire to any ship attempting transit. Since then, Iran has made 21 confirmed attacks on merchant ships. (Wikipedia — 2026 Strait of Hormuz Crisis) Insurance war-risk premiums have been removed for ships in the strait, making the economic cost to ship owners prohibitive.

The Strait is 21 miles wide at its narrowest point and facilitates roughly 20 million barrels of oil per day — approximately 20% of global seaborne oil trade. In 2024, 84% of crude shipments through the strait were destined for Asian markets. China receives a third of its oil via the strait. Europe gets 12–14% of its LNG from Qatar through the strait. (Wikipedia — 2026 Strait of Hormuz Crisis)

⚡ Supply Loss — Verified Numbers

Global supply losses are estimated at roughly 8 million barrels per day as of early April. Coordinated Strategic Petroleum Reserve releases, totaling approximately 426 million barrels, are nearing exhaustion and expected to run out by mid-to-late April. (Newsquawk, Apr 5) Brent has surged about 60% in March alone, peaking near $120/bbl. Analysts at Bloomberg Economics project that if Brent stays near $110, euro area inflation adds roughly 1 percentage point and GDP falls about 0.6%. At $170/bbl, those numbers roughly double. (Bloomberg Graphics, Iran War Oil Shock analysis)

A Financial Times investigation found that $580 million in bets on falling oil prices were placed just 15 minutes before Trump's March 23 statement postponing attacks — generating calls for investigation into potential insider trading. (Wikipedia — Economic Impact of the 2026 Iran War)

Despite being the world's largest oil producer, the U.S. has been affected. California's gasoline prices surged above $5 per gallon during the second week of March 2026. (Wikipedia — Economic Impact) The national average hit $4.08 per gallon by April 2. (NBC News, Apr 2) Trump responded by saying: "If they rise, they rise." (Wikipedia — Economic Impact)

Bloomberg Economics' CPI nowcast for March puts U.S. headline inflation at 3.4% year-over-year — up sharply from 2.4% in February — with rising fuel prices as the primary driver. (Bloomberg Graphics) The OECD projects U.S. full-year 2026 inflation at 4.2%. (Wikipedia — Economic Impact)

04 — Federal Reserve

The Policy Trap the Fed Cannot Escape

The Federal Reserve held the benchmark federal funds rate at 3.50–3.75% at its March 18 meeting — its second hold in 2026. The only dissenter was Governor Miran, who favored a 25bps rate cut. The FOMC statement added new language: "developments in the Middle East pose uncertain implications for the U.S. economy." (Newsquawk · Al Jazeera · CNBC, Mar 18)

The March Summary of Economic Projections shifted in a hawkish direction: growth forecasts were raised for 2026–2028, inflation projections were revised higher — especially for 2026 — and the longer-run fed funds estimate was nudged up to 3.1%. The median dot plot still points to one cut in 2026 and one more in 2027. But of 19 FOMC participants, seven now expect rates unchanged all year — one more than December. (CNBC, Mar 18)

Powell's press conference was perceived as hawkish: he stressed that persistent inflation — not weak growth — remains the main concern. He cited sticky non-housing services, the need for more goods disinflation, and upside risks from tariffs, oil, and the Middle East. (Newsquawk)

📋 This Week's Fed Event — FOMC Minutes (Wednesday Apr 8)

The minutes from the March 18 meeting will be released Wednesday. Key watch items: (1) How explicitly did FOMC members discuss the Iran war's inflation pass-through? (2) Was the threshold for a rate hike discussed? (3) Any dissent on the hawkish tone of the projections? Bond futures briefly priced a 52% probability of a rate hike by end-2026 last week before that pulled back. The minutes could either revive or eliminate that hike risk depending on the language. (CNBC · 10-Year Treasury yield article, Mar 31)

The strong March jobs report — 178,000 added vs. 59,000 expected — now firmly removes any pressure on the Fed to cut. "The March report alleviates pressure on the Federal Reserve to act immediately and instead lets them look ahead to prepare for the impacts of the U.S.-Iran war and rising energy prices," said Glassdoor's chief economist Daniel Zhao. (CBS News, Apr 3) Multiple economists now forecast zero cuts in 2026. (Prism News · CBS News)

06 — The Big Picture

What This Market Actually Is Right Now

This is not a broken market. It is a hostage market. NQ has fallen roughly 10–12% from its February highs — the Dow entered correction territory, down 10% from its February 10 record. The Nasdaq and S&P 500 are each in correction territory as well, with the Nasdaq set for its worst month in a year as of the March 30 close. (CNN, Mar 30 · CNBC) But underneath the geopolitical noise, fundamentals are not collapsing.

S&P 500 earnings are expected to grow roughly 13% in Q1 2026 — what would be the sixth consecutive quarter of double-digit gains, per FactSet data. AI infrastructure spending has not stopped — OpenAI's $852B valuation round closed this week. The March jobs report just printed nearly triple expectations. The consumer is stressed by $4+ gas but has not broken. This is stagflationary, not recessionary — a very different setup from 2008. (Northeastern University · CNBC)

🧠 The Trade Framework for This Environment

Every major market move since late February has been binary: Iran de-escalation = rally, Iran escalation = selloff. Tuesday's 3.83% Nasdaq surge on a single Trump comment tells you the upside velocity when resolution comes. Thursday's 2.2% opening drop — reversed to flat — tells you the downside is also contained if you're patient. The edge in this environment is not predicting resolution. It is trading the volatility the binary creates. Fade the extremes. Respect the TBR. Execute at the windows.

When this war ends — and it will end — the initial NQ rally will be sharp and fast. The 10-year Treasury yield will drop, rate cut expectations will rapidly re-price, and growth equities will lead. The setup for that trade is already visible in the Tuesday session. The only question is timing. Until then, every session is a range trade inside a larger binary event.
