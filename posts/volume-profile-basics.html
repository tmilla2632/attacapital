Volume Profile Basics:
How to Trade VAH, VAL & POC

ATTA Capital April 2026 8 min read

Most traders look at a chart and see candles. What they miss is the story underneath — where volume actually traded. Volume profile flips the script. Instead of plotting volume across time, it plots it across price. The result is a map of where the market spent the most energy, where it found agreement, and where it avoided. That map gives you levels that actually mean something.

This is the foundation of how ATTA Capital reads NQ. Not lines drawn on vibes — levels backed by actual traded volume. Here is everything you need to understand the three core levels: POC, VAH, and VAL.

The Three Levels

POC
Point of Control
The single price level where the most volume traded during the session. The market's true anchor. Price gravitates back to POC — it acts as a magnet when price moves too far away.
VAH
Value Area High
The upper boundary of the Value Area — the top edge of where 70% of volume traded. Acts as resistance when price approaches from below. Becomes support if price breaks and closes above.
VAL
Value Area Low
The lower boundary of the Value Area. Acts as support when price approaches from above. Becomes resistance if price breaks and closes below. A key line in the sand for bulls.

Together, these three levels define the Value Area — the price range where 70% of the session's volume occurred. Think of it as the market's fair value zone. Price inside the Value Area is accepted. Price outside it is either rejected and pulled back, or it breaks with conviction and establishes a new range.

Why 70%?

The 70% figure comes from market profile theory, originally developed by Peter Steidlmayer at the CME. The logic is simple: if the majority of participants transacted within a given price range, that range represents consensus value. Price outside that range is either cheap or expensive relative to where the market agreed to trade. Those inefficiencies tend to resolve — either by price returning to value, or by enough new participants stepping in to shift what "value" means.

The market is always searching for price levels where both buyers and sellers are willing to transact. Volume profile shows you exactly where that agreement happened — and where it did not.

How to Trade Each Level

Understanding what the levels are is half the battle. The other half is knowing what to do when price arrives at them. Here is how each one behaves and the setups they generate.

// POC — The Magnet

When price trades away from POC and then returns, it often consolidates or reverses at POC. If you are in a trending move that has pushed far from the prior session's POC, expect a mean reversion back toward it. POC is also a key intraday target — when price is sitting between VAL and VAH, it tends to gravitate toward POC before making a larger directional move.

// VAL — The Floor

When price pulls back to VAL from inside the Value Area, watch for a long setup with a close confirmation. VAL acts as a floor. If price bounces from VAL, bulls are defending value. If price breaks VAL and closes below it, the structure has shifted — that same VAL now becomes resistance on any retrace, and the next target is the profile low or the VAL of the prior session.

// VAH — The Ceiling

VAH is where sellers tend to show up. When price approaches VAH from below, look for rejection and a fade back toward POC or VAL. If price breaks and closes above VAH, the dynamic flips — VAH becomes support and the move can extend to the profile high or the prior session's VAH. A clean break above VAH with volume is one of the highest-conviction continuation signals in volume profile trading.

The 80% Rule

One of the most widely used rules in value area trading is the 80% Rule. The premise: if price opens outside the prior day's Value Area and then enters it, there is roughly an 80% probability that price will travel all the way through the Value Area to the opposite boundary.

// 80% Rule — How It Works

If price opens below VAL and rallies up through VAL, the 80% rule says it is likely to reach VAH. If price opens above VAH and drops through it, it is likely to reach VAL. This is not a guarantee — it is a probability bias that helps you set realistic targets and manage trades with context rather than guessing.

Scenario Playbook

Here is a quick reference for the most common volume profile scenarios and how to approach them:

Scenario What It Means Bias
Price opens inside VA, tests VAL, holds Buyers defending value — mean reversion trade toward POC or VAH LONG
Price opens inside VA, tests VAH, rejects Sellers defending upper value — fade back toward POC or VAL SHORT
Price breaks and closes above VAH Acceptance above value — VAH becomes support, target profile high LONG
Price breaks and closes below VAL Acceptance below value — VAL becomes resistance, target profile low SHORT
Price far from POC, trending session Mean reversion toward POC likely before next directional leg WAIT
Opens outside VA, enters VA 80% rule in play — high probability of reaching opposite boundary DIRECTIONAL

Close Confirmation — Non-Negotiable

One of the biggest mistakes traders make with volume profile levels is reacting to price touching a level rather than waiting for price to close relative to it. A wick through VAH means nothing. A close above VAH on the 1-minute or 5-minute is what confirms acceptance.

At ATTA Capital the rule is simple: wait for close confirmation before entering on any volume profile level. Price that touches and rejects is a test. Price that closes on the other side is a break. Trading the touch without confirmation is how you get chopped out of otherwise valid setups.

A level is only broken when price closes on the other side of it. Wicks are noise. Closes are conviction. Wait for the close — every time.

Stacking With Other Tools

Volume profile levels are powerful alone but sharper in confluence. At ATTA Capital these levels are always layered with EMA structure and TBR (Time Based Range) levels. When a VAL lines up with a TBR support and the 9 EMA is holding above price, the probability of a clean bounce increases significantly. When a VAH aligns with a TBR resistance and EMAs are fanning down, the short setup becomes much higher conviction.

The market does not move in a vacuum. Volume profile gives you the where. Structure and momentum give you the when. Confluence of all three is where the cleanest setups live.

The Bottom Line

Volume profile is not a magic indicator. It is a map of where the market transacted — and like any map, it only helps if you know how to read it. POC is the anchor. VAH and VAL are the boundaries. Price inside the zone is in balance. Price outside it is in discovery. Your job is to identify which state the market is in and position accordingly — with patience, with confirmation, and with a clear plan before price ever gets to the level.

That is the mindset. That is the approach. Reset. Refocus. Restart — every single session.
